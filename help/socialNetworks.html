Social Networks

Social Network graphs (also called chord diagrams) are helpful for showing relationships of collaboration between many parties. In our case, the parties are scholars, and the relationships are either publishing a paper together or investigating a grant together by being co-PIs.

Some terminology has been defined to ease with communicating about these:

  • The “network” is either all of the scholars or the cohort of scholars you’ve selected.
  • A “collaborator” is a scholar in the network or, if requested, a mentor of a scholar.
  • Collaborations are directional. Citations are “received from” a scholar, “given to” another collaborator (a scholar or mentor). In the graph, mentors cannot initiate a collaboration because their publications are not tracked.
  • A “connection” is one publication/grant made together between two collaborators in the network. A connection must be begun by a scholar (“received from”) and “given to” a collaborator (either a scholar or mentor). (Two mentors cannot connect to each other. Mentors’ publications and grants are not tracked.) Graphically, a connection is a directional edge between two nodes.
  • The total connections is the sum total of papers co-authored between a scholar and a collaborator, for each scholar in the network. Graphically, it is the total number of directional edges in a graph.
  • Superlatives are outstanding individuals in your network. These are noted in the statistics. Outstanding metrics are the maximum number of collaborators and the maximum number of connections.
  • Authorship position or position in the list of a grant is not considered in the social network graph.

Finally, a bar chart is provided to chart the number of connections over time. The x-axis is year, and the y-axis is the number of connections.
